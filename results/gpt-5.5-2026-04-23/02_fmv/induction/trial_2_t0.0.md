1. **Motion requires a continuing push or pull.**
   An object that is not being pushed, pulled, carried, or made to fall stays where it is. If it was moving only because of a hand or carrier, it halts the instant that help stops.

2. **A steady effort gives a steady pace.**
   When a hand gives a block one unchanging pull, the block moves at one unchanging pace. Keeping the same pull for a longer time does not make the block gather speed.

3. **The full pace begins at once.**
   As soon as the pull begins, the block is already moving at the pace that belongs to that pull. There is no slow build-up from rest.

4. **The pace ends at once when the effort ends.**
   When the pull or push is released, the object stops immediately at that place. It does not drift, coast, or carry itself onward.

5. **Pace is directly matched to effort.**
   For the same block in the same situation, twice the pull gives twice the pace. Three times the pull gives three times the pace. In general, more steady effort gives a matching greater steady pace.

6. **Heavier objects answer less to the same effort.**
   If one block is twice as heavy as another, the same steady pull moves the heavier block at half the pace of the lighter one. A heavier load therefore needs more effort to keep the same pace as a lighter load.

7. **The track is not the cause of the stopping.**
   A block in open space behaves the same way as on the level track: a steady push gives a steady pace, and releasing the push makes the block stop at once. This rule holds even when the block touches nothing else.

8. **Carrying a load requires effort for the whole trip.**
   A carrier must keep pushing or carrying the load all the way from one place to another. Pushing hard only at the start cannot make the load continue by itself.

9. **Side-by-side efforts add.**
   If two carriers push the same block in the same direction, the block’s pace is the sum of the paces each carrier would produce alone. Two equal helpers therefore make it move twice as fast as one helper, for the same block.

10. **Opposed efforts subtract.**
   If two carriers push equally hard in opposite directions, the block stays put. If one pushes harder, only the extra part of that effort counts, and the block moves in the stronger carrier’s direction at the pace belonging to that difference.

11. **Sideways carrying does not continue after release.**
   A stone carried forward by a moving hand moves forward only while the hand holds or pushes it. The instant the hand lets go, the forward motion ends. The stone then falls straight down from the release point.

12. **Falling is a built-in downward motion with a fixed pace.**
   When an object is released above the ground, it falls downward at one steady, unchanging pace. The pace of falling does not increase during the fall.

13. **Falling pace does not depend on heaviness.**
   A boulder and a pebble released together from the same height fall side by side and strike the ground together. Their different heaviness does not change their downward falling pace.

14. **Air does not set the falling pace.**
   Objects fall at the same steady pace in open air and in a tall jar with the air removed. The presence or absence of air makes no difference to the falling described here.

15. **Separate directions are handled separately.**
   A forward push gives forward motion only while it continues. Falling gives downward motion after release. Thus a stone let go from a moving hand loses its forward motion at once and drops straight down.

**Remaining tension:**

Ordinary pushes and pulls make pace depend on effort and heaviness, yet falling has one fixed pace for both heavy and light objects and does not need a continuing hand.
