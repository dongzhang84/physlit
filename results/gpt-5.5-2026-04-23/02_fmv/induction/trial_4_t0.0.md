3. **Pace is directly matched to effort.**
   For the same object, twice the effort gives twice the pace, three times the effort gives three times the pace, and so on.

4. **Heaviness resists pace in direct proportion.**
   For the same effort, a block twice as heavy moves at half the pace. More generally, the pace is larger when the effort is larger and smaller when the object is heavier.

5. **A useful rule for carried or pulled objects is:**
   the pace equals the applied effort divided by the object’s heaviness.
   This gives a steady pace immediately, with no gradual change from rest.

6. **The track is not the cause of stopping.**
   The same rules hold on a level track and in open space. An object does not keep going merely because nothing is touching it. It keeps moving only while a push or pull is being supplied.

7. **Travel requires continuing effort.**
   To move a load from one place to another, the carrier must keep pushing or pulling throughout the journey. A hard push at the start only moves the load during that push; once the push ends, the load halts.

8. **Pushes in the same direction add.**
   If two carriers push the same block side by side in the same direction, their efforts combine. The block’s pace is the same as if one carrier supplied the sum of their efforts.

9. **Opposed pushes subtract.**
   If two carriers push in opposite directions, equal efforts cancel and the object stays put. If one effort is stronger, the object moves in the direction of the stronger effort, at the pace belonging to the leftover effort after the weaker one is subtracted.

10. **Falling is caused by a constant downward pull on each object.**
   Every released object is pulled downward with a steady strength suited to its heaviness, so that all released objects fall at the same steady pace. A boulder and a pebble therefore fall side by side and reach the ground together.

11. **Falling pace does not build up.**
   The downward pull of falling gives its full falling pace instantly and keeps that pace unchanged until the object reaches the ground or is otherwise stopped.

12. **Letting go removes the hand’s forward push.**
   A stone carried forward by a moving hand has forward motion only because the hand is carrying it. When the hand lets go, the forward push is gone, so the stone has no reason to continue forward. It then responds only to the downward pull and drops straight down from the release point.

13. **Air does not set the falling pace.**
   Since objects fall at the same steady pace in open air and in a jar with the air removed, the presence or absence of air is not part of the basic falling rule.

14. **All motion is the moment-by-moment result of the current unbalanced push or pull.**
   Add all pushes and pulls along a line, counting opposite directions against each other. If something is left over, the object moves that way at a pace set by the leftover effort and by the object’s heaviness. If nothing is left over, it stays put.
