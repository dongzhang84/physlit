It gave examples and rankings for iron, stone, metal, water, smoke, flame, dry oak floating in water, and feather-down drifting in air. It did not establish that hollow wood dropped through air must fall slower than iron.

I justified it by saying:

> “The hollow wooden ball, being lighter/airier in character and less strongly downward-tending than iron, is expected to be more delayed by the surrounding air.”

But Stage 2’s Rule 2 emphasized shape and medium:

> “A compact body descends faster than a broad, thin, spread-out body of equal weight.”

Here the objects are described as balls, so shape may be similar. Their weights are not specified. I imported an assumption that hollow wood is “lighter/airier” and therefore more delayed. That is plausible inside the broad framework, but it was not tightly operationalized.

So the gap is: **the rules did not specify how to rank a hollow wooden ball versus a solid iron ball of possibly similar size, nor did they provide a rule connecting hollowness or woodiness to fall time in air except by analogy.**

### Scenario 2: cart given one quick push on perfectly smooth ice

### Scenario 3: two same-sized stones below water, one twice as weighty

This follows partly but not completely. Stage 2 says stones sink, and Rule 2 says the surrounding medium hinders passage. However, the operational comparison most directly stated was:

The qualitative prediction follows if “more weighty” means “stronger downward character,” but that mapping was not explicitly operationalized. So the gap is smaller than in scenario 1 or 2, but still present: **Stage 2 did not give an exact operational rule that same-sized stones of different weights sink at different speeds.**

**Q4. How does this framework differ from standard physics, without naming the framework?**

The framework differs from standard physics in several major ways.

1. **Motion is explained by natural places and body-kinds, not by universal forces and acceleration laws.**

In the used framework, stones, iron, water, smoke, and flame have characteristic directions because of what kind of thing they are:

> “earth and water belong below; air and smoke-like or flame-like stuff belong above.”

Standard physics would not say iron falls because its kind naturally belongs below, or smoke rises because smoky stuff belongs above. It would analyze gravitational force, buoyancy, pressure gradients, density, drag, and fluid flow.

2. **Continued motion is treated as needing continued urging, except for special temporary cases.**

For carts I wrote:

> “continued travel requires continued urging by a living mover or other source of push or pull.”

and:

> “No continued urging → motion fades and stops.”

Standard physics would instead distinguish between force causing acceleration and friction causing deceleration. A cart does not need a continuing force to keep moving at constant velocity in the absence of resistance. The framework I used treats rest as the ordinary outcome when the mover is gone.

3. **Projectile motion is explained by a fading received motion rather than inertia plus gravity.**

For arrows I wrote:

> “the received forward flight from the bow… fades with passage through air.”

and:

> “As the received forward flight fades, the downward tendency becomes more apparent.”

Standard physics would say the arrow retains horizontal velocity except insofar as air resistance slows it, while gravity accelerates it downward throughout. The framework I used treats forward motion as an imparted temporary condition that naturally decays.

4. **Media are treated qualitatively as hindering substances rather than through quantitative laws.**

I used rankings such as:

> “air < water < honey”

and comparative rules like:

> “Greater hindrance of medium → slower passage.”

Standard physics would model drag, viscosity, density, Reynolds number, buoyant force, terminal velocity, and shape-dependent fluid dynamics. The framework here has qualitative rankings but no equations.

5. **Celestial motion is governed by a separate rule from terrestrial motion.**

I wrote:

> “The Sun, Moon, and fixed stars belong to an upper region whose regular motion is circular rather than upward or downward.”

and:

> “They are borne around once each day.”

Standard physics applies the same mechanics universally to terrestrial and celestial bodies. This framework divides the world into terrestrial motions—up, down, forced, hindered—and heavenly circular motion.
