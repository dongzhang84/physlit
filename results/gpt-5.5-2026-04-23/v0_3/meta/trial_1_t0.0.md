**Q4. Without naming the framework, what are the main differences from standard physics?**

The framework I used differs from standard physics in several consequential ways.

### 1. Motion is explained by natural destinations or tendencies, not by forces and acceleration

In the framework, stones fall because earthy things naturally go downward:

> “Earthy and metallic things … tend downward toward the earth.”

Smoke and flame rise because smoky or fiery things naturally go upward:

> “Smoky, fiery, and flame-like things tend upward toward the sky.”

Standard physics would instead explain falling through gravity, buoyancy, pressure gradients, and fluid dynamics. It would not assign different natural destinations to different material kinds in this way.

### 2. Continued projectile motion requires a fading imparted motion

I wrote:

> “a bow gives an arrow forward motion that persists for many paces after contact ends”

and:

> “that imparted motion fades.”

In standard physics, the arrow’s horizontal motion does not need a continuing internal “imparted motion” to sustain it. Inertia explains persistence of motion; forces such as drag and gravity change the motion. The framework I used treats continued forced motion as something that must be temporarily carried by the body and then exhausted.

### 3. Heavier bodies are expected to fall faster through air because they better overcome hindrance

I wrote:

> “If two falling bodies are similar in compactness but one is heavier, the heavier one falls faster through air.”

Standard physics gives a more specific account: in vacuum, bodies fall with the same gravitational acceleration regardless of mass; in air, terminal velocity and drag depend on mass, shape, area, density, and speed. Heavier same-shape objects may fall faster in air, but not because heaviness is a simple natural-speed principle.

### 4. Terrestrial and celestial motions are governed by separate rules

I wrote:

> “The Sun, Moon, and fixed stars belong to the sky and naturally move in daily circles around the world.”

and:

> “Their circular paths are not the same kind of motion as stones, smoke, arrows, carts, or rain near the earth.”

Standard physics does not use fundamentally separate laws for heavenly bodies and earthly bodies. It treats both under the same mechanics and gravitation, even though the motions can appear very different.

### 5. Rest is treated as the default end of forced terrestrial motion

In the framework:

> “If the pushing or pulling ceases, the unnatural motion fades.”

and:

> “a cart … comes to rest.”

In standard physics, uniform motion can persist without a continued push if no net force acts. Rest is not more natural than uniform straight-line motion. The framework instead treats non-natural sideways motion as something that decays unless maintained.
