**Q4. How does this framework differ from standard physics?**

Without naming the historical framework, the main differences are these:

### 1. Motion is explained by natural directions or places, not by forces and acceleration.

In my framework, bodies have inherent tendencies:

> “iron remains downward-seeking; smoke remains upward-seeking”

and:

> “each kind of stuff has a natural place or region.”

Standard physics would instead describe gravitational acceleration, buoyancy, pressure gradients, combustion products, fluid flow, and so on. It would not say that stone “belongs” below or smoke “belongs” above in the same basic explanatory sense.

### 2. Continued horizontal motion is treated as needing a continuing mover, except for temporary carried motion.

I wrote:

> “A thing moving along a level road continues only while the mover continues to act on it”

and:

> “no continued mover → motion fades and stops.”

Standard physics would say that, absent net external force, horizontal motion continues uniformly. A cart stops on a road because of friction and other resistive forces, not because motion itself requires a continuing mover.

### 3. Projectile motion is explained by fading carried motion plus persistent downward tendency.

I wrote:

> “the carried forward motion fades”

and:

> “as the carried forward motion fades, the arrow’s natural downward tendency becomes more evident.”

Standard physics would instead say that, neglecting air resistance, the horizontal component of velocity remains constant while gravity accelerates the arrow downward from the start. The forward motion does not need an internal “carried motion” that decays, except insofar as air drag reduces speed.

### 4. Falling speed is ranked by heaviness and compactness rather than by gravitational acceleration plus resistance.

I wrote:

> “a compact heavy body descends more readily than a light or spread-out body”

and:

> “an iron ball descends faster than a dried pea.”

Standard physics distinguishes gravitational acceleration from air resistance. In vacuum, an iron ball, pea, and feather would all have the same gravitational acceleration near Earth. Differences in air are due to drag, mass, shape, cross-sectional area, and terminal velocity, not an intrinsic heavier-falls-faster tendency.

### 5. Celestial motion is put in a separate category from terrestrial motion.

I wrote:

> “The lights in the sky naturally move in daily circles around the world.”

and:

> “This is a separate rule because their observed motion is neither ordinary downward motion... nor upward motion...”

Standard physics uses one mechanics for terrestrial and celestial bodies, with gravitation applying to both. The apparent daily motion of Sun, Moon, and stars is chiefly explained by Earth’s rotation, not by the heavenly lights naturally circling the world once per day.

### 6. Floating and sinking are classificatory rather than quantitative.

I wrote:

> “stone: below water”

> “dry oak: at water surface”

Standard physics would explain this by density, buoyant force, displaced fluid, shape, trapped air, and equilibrium. My framework treated the water-relation as an observed classification rather than a general quantitative principle.
