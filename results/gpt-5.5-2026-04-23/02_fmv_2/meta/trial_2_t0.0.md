In standard physics near Earth, free-fall speed increases with time under gravitational acceleration, so fall time grows like the square root of height, not linearly with height.

5. **Gravity is treated as a special rule giving equal pace, not as a force producing acceleration proportional to gravitational mass/inertial mass**

I wrote:

> “falling is produced by a steady downward pull giving equal downward pace to all released bodies”

and:

> “This falling rule is a special rule because it overrides the ordinary heaviness dependence.”

In standard physics, gravity produces equal acceleration for different masses because gravitational and inertial mass scale together, not because all bodies are assigned the same fixed downward speed.

**Estimate: minor.**

The main Stage 3 predictions were largely governed by the nonstandard Stage 2 rules:
