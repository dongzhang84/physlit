# Proposed Rules

1. **Every body has a natural region toward which it tends to travel when nothing holds it back.** Heavy things (stone, iron, water) tend toward the earth and seek to come to rest upon or within it. Light things (smoke, flame) tend upward, away from the earth, regardless of how their source is oriented. (Explains 3, 4, 5-stone, 8, and the downward tendency in 1, 4, 7, 11, 12.)

2. **A body's eagerness to reach its natural region is proportional to its weight, and is resisted by the medium it must pass through.** A denser medium (honey, water) resists more than a thinner one (air); a body whose weight is too small compared to the resistance of the medium it sits in does not sink at all but rests at the surface (oak on water). (Explains 5, 9, and combined with rule 3, explains 1 and 11.)

3. **The resistance a medium offers to a body depends on how much of the body's surface meets the medium, not only on the body's weight.** A flat sheet meets more air than a compact ball of equal weight; a pea has little weight against the air it must push aside; a feather-down has so little weight against so much surface that the air's own stirrings move it about more than its downward tendency does. (Explains 1, 11, 12, and the slowness in 9.)

4. **A body at rest on the earth stays at rest unless something pushes or pulls it; a body being pushed or pulled moves only while the pushing or pulling continues, and stops shortly after it ceases.** The road and the wagon's load both add to what must be overcome: a heavier load demands harder straining. (Explains 2, 6.)

5. **A projectile, once launched, continues in the direction it was sent for a while, but its travel weakens with distance, and meanwhile rule 1 draws it toward its natural region.** So a horizontal arrow keeps going forward but curves down and lands. (Explains 7.)

6. **The Sun, Moon, and stars belong to the sky and travel along fixed circular tracks there**, distinct from earthly bodies which obey rules 1–5. (Explains 10.)

# Tensions
