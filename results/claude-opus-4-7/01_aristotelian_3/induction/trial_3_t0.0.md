# Proposed Rules

1. **Every body has a natural place toward which it tends to travel when nothing holds it back.** Heavy things (stone, iron, water, the pea, oak as a solid object) tend downward toward the earth; light things (smoke, flame) tend upward toward the sky. This explains 3, 4, 8, and the downward part of 1, 5, 7, 11, 12.

2. **The strength of a body's downward tendency is set by its weight; this same tendency must be overcome by whatever supports or carries it.** A pond's water can support oak but not stone (5); an ox strains harder against a heavier load (6). The empty wagon also requires effort, but less — a special case of the same rule.

3. **A body moving through a surrounding stuff (air, honey, water) must push that stuff aside, and the stuff resists being pushed.** The resistance grows with how much surface the body presents to the stuff and with how thick the stuff is, and it lessens with the body's weight (since a heavier body presses through more insistently).
   - Honey resists more than air, so the pebble settles slowly in honey, quickly in air (9).
   - The flat sheet presents more surface than the compact ball of equal weight, so it is held back more (11).
   - The pea, being very light for its surface, is held back far more than the iron ball (1).
   - Feather-down, extremely light and broad-surfaced, is barely able to push air aside at all, and any motion of the air carries it along (12).

4. **A body at rest on the ground stays at rest; a body set into horizontal motion by a pusher continues only so long as the push continues or some carrying influence remains, and otherwise is brought to rest by the resistance of what it moves through or over.**
   - The cart rolls while pushed and stops when released, slowed by the road and air (2).
   - The arrow, having received a strong launching, carries forward through the air for a while; as the air wears down its forward motion, its natural downward tendency (rule 1) takes over and it strikes the earth (7).

5. **The heavenly bodies — Sun, Moon, fixed stars — move in unending circular paths around the earth, unlike earthly bodies which seek their natural place and then rest.** (10)

# Tensions

- **Observation 2 vs. 7** sits uneasily: the cart stops quickly once the push ends, but the arrow flies on for many paces after leaving the string. Rule 4 papers this over by appealing to "carrying influence" and to differences in how strongly each was launched and how each meets the air, but it does not really explain *why* a brief sharp launching carries an arrow far while a sustained push leaves a cart with no afterglow of motion. A more careful account would need to say what the arrow carries with it that the cart does not.
- **Observation 10** is simply asserted as a separate rule because nothing else in the list constrains heavenly motion; the earthly rules and the heavenly rule are not unified.
