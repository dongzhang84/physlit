2. **The strength of a body's tendency toward its natural place is set by its weight.** A heavier body presses toward its place more strongly than a lighter one. This is why the iron ball outruns the pea (obs. 1), why a stone sinks while oak floats on water (obs. 5 — oak's downward tendency is weaker than water's, so water displaces it), and why an iron bar drops straight while feather-down barely descends at all (obs. 12).

3. **The medium a body travels through resists its passage, and the resistance is greater when the medium is thicker or when the body presents a broader face to it.**
   - A pebble crosses honey slowly but air quickly, because honey is the thicker medium (obs. 9).
   - A flat sheet and a compact ball of equal weight fall differently because the sheet presents a broader face to the air (obs. 11).
   - The pea, being very light, is overcome by air's resistance far more than the iron ball is (obs. 1, also obs. 12 for feather-down, which is so light that even a breath of moving air pushes it sideways).

4. **A body only continues moving along the ground (or otherwise away from its natural place) so long as something keeps pushing or pulling it; once the pushing stops, the resistance of its surroundings brings it to rest.** The cart rolls while pushed and halts when released (obs. 2). The loaded wagon resists the ox more than the empty one because greater weight presses harder against the road and so meets greater resistance (obs. 6).

5. **A push given to a body can persist in it for a while after the pusher lets go, carrying it forward until that impressed push is worn away by the resistance of the medium and the body's own downward tendency reasserts itself.** This is why the arrow flies forward many paces after leaving the string before curving down to earth (obs. 7). It is consistent with rule 4: the cart on the ground loses its impressed push very quickly because the road resists it strongly; the arrow in open air loses it slowly.
