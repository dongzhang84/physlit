# Proposed Rules

1. **Every body has a natural place toward which it tends to travel when nothing holds it back.** Heavy stuffs (stone, iron, water) belong below, on or under the earth; airy and fiery stuffs (smoke, flame) belong above. A body left to itself moves toward its natural place along a straight vertical path. (Explains 3, 4, 5-stone, 8, 10 is not covered here; 12-iron.)

2. **The heavenly bodies — Sun, Moon, fixed stars — are of a different kind from earthly bodies, and their natural motion is circular and unending around the earth, not straight up or down.** (Explains 10.)

3. **A body travels toward its natural place more vigorously the heavier it is, and more sluggishly the lighter it is.** Hence between two released bodies, the heavier strikes ground sooner. (Explains 1, and combined with rule 4 explains 11, 12.)

4. **The medium through which a body moves (honey, water, air) resists its passage, and resists more strongly the more the body's shape spreads against the medium and the less weight the body has to press through.** Thicker media resist more than thinner ones; the same body settles slowly in honey, quickly in air. A flat sheet meets more of the medium than a compact ball of the same weight, so it is held back more. A pea or feather-down, having little weight to press through the air, is easily held up or pushed about by the slightest stirring of that air. (Explains 1, 9, 11, 12, and the floating oak in 5: wood is so light for its spread that water fully holds it up.)

5. **A body does not move along the ground or through the air of its own accord unless it is traveling toward its natural place. To keep such a body in any other motion, something must keep urging it; once the urging stops, the medium and the ground wear the motion away and the body halts (or, if aloft, resumes falling toward its natural place).** The heavier the load being urged, the harder the urger must strain to keep it going. (Explains 2, 6, and the horizontal stretch of 7.)

6. **A thrown or shot body carries with it, for a while, the urging given by the thrower or bowstring; this borrowed urging fades as the body travels, and once it is spent the body's natural downward tendency takes over and curves its path to the earth.** (Special application of rule 5 to projectiles; explains 7.)

# Tensions

Rule 6 requires a "borrowed urging" that persists after contact with the bowstring ends, which sits uncomfortably with rule 5's claim that non-natural motion needs continuous urging. I have patched this by saying the urging is *imparted* and then *fades*, but the mechanism by which a departed hand or string continues to urge is not explained by the observations themselves — it is posited to save the appearance.

- **Observation 10** is handled only by declaring the heavens a separate kind of thing (rule 2). The observations give no bridge between earthly and heavenly behavior, so this remains a stipulation rather than a derivation.
