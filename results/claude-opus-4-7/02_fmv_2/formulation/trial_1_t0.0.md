**2. Pace is set by effort divided by heaviness.**
   1. *Operational form:* While one or more pushes act on a body along a single line, combine them into a net effort by adding pushes in the same direction and subtracting pushes in the opposite direction. Then the body's pace along that line equals (net effort) ÷ (heaviness). Doubling the net effort doubles the pace; doubling the heaviness halves the pace; if the net effort is zero, the pace is zero.
   2. *Scope:* Applies whenever effort is being applied to a body. Covers pushes along the track, combined pushes from two sources, and the downward pull of rule 3. Requires that the efforts be resolved along the line of motion under consideration.
   3. *What changes / what stays the same:* While the effort and heaviness are held fixed, the pace is fixed — it does not grow or shrink with time. The body's position changes steadily; its pace does not. Heaviness is a property of the body and does not change as the body moves.
   4. *Boundary cases:* The rule presumes effort and heaviness can be compared as magnitudes (twice as heavy, half as much push). The observations support this only by ranking and doubling; finer ratios are an extrapolation.

**3. Downward pull is proportional to heaviness.**
   1. *Operational form:* Every body is subject to a downward pull whose magnitude equals its heaviness (in the same units in which heaviness enters rule 2). Applying rule 2: downward pace = heaviness ÷ heaviness = one fixed downward pace, the same for every body, at every moment of falling.
