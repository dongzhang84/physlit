## Rule 1 — Motion requires an active push or pull

1. **Operational form.** At every instant, check whether any push or pull is acting on the body. If none is acting, the body's pace is zero (it occupies the same place at the next instant as at this one). ## Rule 2 — Pace is set by effort divided by heaviness

1. **Operational form.** For a single push or pull of effort *E* acting on a body of heaviness *H*, the resulting pace is
   `pace = E / H`,
   in the direction the push is directed. This pace is present in full from the first instant the push acts and does not change while *E* and *H* are unchanged. If *E* doubles, pace doubles; if *H* doubles, pace halves. Two bodies under the same *E* but with heaviness in ratio 1:2 will have paces in ratio 2:1.

2. **Scope.** Applies whenever exactly one push or pull acts on the body. When more than one acts, Rule 3 combines them first; the combined effort is then divided by *H*. Applies on tracks and in open space alike.

3. **What changes, what stays the same.** What changes: location, as long as the push acts. What stays the same: the ratio *E/H* — and therefore the pace — is constant for as long as *E* and *H* are unchanged. Heaviness *H* is treated as a fixed property of the body.

4. **Boundary cases.** The rule does not specify what happens in the single instant of transition when *E* changes from one value to another; we assume the new pace appears immediately, matching the "full pace from the first instant" clause.

5. No imported concepts.

## Rule 3 — Combination of simultaneous pushes

1. **Operational form.** When several pushes or pulls act at once on one body, first compute the pace each would produce alone (Rule 2: its own effort divided by the body's heaviness). Then combine these paces by direction:
   - Paces in the same direction: add them.
   - Paces in opposing directions: subtract the smaller from the larger; the body moves at the difference, in the direction of the larger.
   - If opposing paces are equal: the body stays put.

   Equivalently, since heaviness is shared, one may sum the efforts by direction first and then divide by *H*; the result is the same.

2. **Scope.** Applies whenever two or more pushes or pulls act on the same body at the same instant. Includes the case where the world's downward pull (Rule 4) acts together with another push.

3. **What changes, what stays the same.** What changes: location, in the direction of the net side. What stays the same: as long as the set of pushes and their efforts are unchanged, the combined pace is unchanged. Equal opposing pushes produce no motion at all — there is no stored strain or accumulation.

4. **Boundary cases.** The rules as stated handle only pushes that are either aligned or directly opposed. Pushes at an angle to one another are not covered by any observation and the rule is silent on them.

## Rule 4 — The world's downward pull

1. **Operational form.** When nothing holds a body up, the world exerts a downward pull on it whose effort is exactly proportional to that body's heaviness. That is, for the world's pull, `E_world = k · H` for some fixed strength *k* the same for all bodies. By Rule 2, the resulting downward pace is
   `pace_down = (k · H) / H = k`,

2. **Scope.** Applies to every body not currently held up by contact, a track, or another push that opposes the downward pull. Applies in air and in emptied space alike. Does not apply while the body is supported (in which case the support cancels the world's pull via Rule 3).

3. **What changes, what stays the same.** What changes: the body's height, at the steady rate *k*. What stays the same: the downward pace itself — it does not grow as the fall continues — and the matching of pull-effort to heaviness, which is why the pace is the same for all bodies.
