# Rules for this world

1. **An object moves only while it is being pushed or pulled. The moment all effort on it stops, it halts on the spot.** This covers observations 2, 3, 6, 7, and 11: motion starts instantly with the push, ends instantly with its release, and a thrown stone simply drops where the hand let go.

2. **While effort is applied, the object's pace is fixed by the rule: pace = (net effort) ÷ (heaviness).** That is, doubling the effort doubles the pace; doubling the heaviness halves the pace; and when two efforts act on one object, only their combined push along the line of motion counts (same direction adds, opposite directions subtract). This single rule covers observations 1, 4, 5, 8, and 9.

3. **Every object, whatever its heaviness, is pulled downward by an effort exactly in proportion to its own heaviness.** Combined with rule 2, the downward pace of a falling object works out the same for every object — heavier things are pulled harder but are correspondingly harder to move — so a boulder and a pebble fall together at one steady, unchanging pace (observation 10). The surrounding air plays no role in the pull, so removing it changes nothing (observation 12).

4. **The track, the open space far from anything, and the air a stone passes through exert no push or pull of their own on a moving object.** So a block on the track behaves the same as a block adrift in empty space (observation 6), and air neither helps nor hinders a fall (observations 11, 12).

# Tension

- **Observation 11** is the most delicate. When the stone is being carried, the hand is pushing it forward (giving it its forward pace) and also supporting it against the downward pull. At the instant of release, *both* of those efforts vanish together. By rule 1 the forward motion must halt at once; by rule 3 the downward pull takes over immediately and the stone descends at its steady falling pace. The rules give the right behavior, but only if one accepts that the carrying hand was actively supplying the forward push the whole time it held the stone — there is no "leftover" forward push stored in the stone itself.
