# Proposed Rules

1. **Every material has a natural region toward which it tends to move when nothing holds it back.** Heavy materials (stone, iron, water) have their natural region at the earth below; light materials (smoke, flame, hot air) have their natural region in the sky above. A body left to itself travels toward its natural region along the straight line connecting it to that region (straight down for heavy things, straight up for light things). This explains observations 1, 3, 4, 5 (stone sinks, oak is light relative to water and so stays on top), 8 (flame seeks upward regardless of candle orientation), and 12 (iron falls straight down).

2. **The surrounding medium (air, water, honey) resists the passage of a body through it, and this resistance is greater for bodies that present more of themselves to the medium relative to their heaviness.** A very heavy body of small spread brushes the medium aside easily; a light or spread-out body is held up and dawdled by it. This explains:
   - 1 (pea is light and yields to air more than the iron ball),
   - 9 (honey resists far more than air does, so the same pebble settles slowly through honey),
   - 11 (the hammered sheet presents a broad face to the air; the ball of equal heaviness presents little),
   - 12 (feather-down is so light and so spread that the air's least stirring carries it sideways, while the iron bar pushes straight through).
   - It also covers why the oak in 5 is held at the surface: water resists the oak's downward tendency more strongly than the oak's slight heaviness can overcome.

3. **A body only continues moving in a direction other than toward its natural region while something keeps pushing it; remove the pusher and the body slows and stops, then resumes seeking its natural region.** The cart in 2 rolls only while the hands push, and stops once released (the road's roughness also opposes its motion, as in rule 2). The wagon in 6 needs the ox's straining because the heavier load presses harder on the road and resists being dragged more. This rule, combined with rule 1, also explains 7: the bowstring imparts a forward push to the arrow; the air carries that push along for some distance (sustaining the horizontal flight), but the push fades, and meanwhile the arrow's heaviness is always drawing it earthward, so its path curves down and it lands.

4. **The heavenly bodies are of a different nature from earthly materials: their proper motion is uniform circular travel around the earth, not motion toward an earthly region.** This is a separate rule because nothing in rules 1–3 would produce the daily repeating circles of observation 10; the Sun, Moon, and stars do not fall, do not slow, and do not seek the ground.

# Tensions

- **Observation 7 (the arrow)** is the most strained. Rule 3 says a body stops when its pusher is removed, yet the arrow flies on for many paces after leaving the string. I patched this by saying the air carries the push along, but I have not actually shown *why* air would do so when in every other case (cart, wagon, falling sheet) the air and ground only *resist* motion. The arrow seems to require the medium to act as both sustainer and resister, which is awkward.
- **Observation 10 (heavens)** is handled only by declaring the heavens a separate kind of thing. The rules give no unified account of why earthly and heavenly matter should behave so differently; rule 4 is essentially a concession rather than an explanation.
