# Operational Rules

1. **Rule of Natural Place (operational).**
   Every earthly body has a natural place determined by its kind:
   - Earthy bodies (stone, iron, pebble, oak): natural place is the ground (lowest).
   - Water: natural place is above earth but below air.
   - Air: natural place is above water but below fire.
   - Fiery/smoky bodies (flame, smoke): natural place is highest.
   When nothing pushes or holds a body, it travels in a straight line toward its natural place: earthy and watery bodies move downward, fiery and smoky bodies move upward. The direction does not depend on the orientation of the source (a tilted candle still sends flame upward).
   **Scope:** earthly bodies only; applies when no push is impressed and no obstacle blocks the path.
   **Preserved:** the body's kind, and therefore the direction of its natural tendency, are preserved throughout the motion. Position changes; tendency-direction does not.
   **Boundary:** does not say what happens when a body has already reached its natural place (presumably it rests); does not address mixed bodies.

2. **Rule of Weight-Ranking (operational).**
   Among bodies traveling toward their natural place through the *same* medium and presenting *similar* faces to it, rank them by weight: the heavier body arrives sooner than the lighter, and a heavier body sinks through a lighter medium that a lighter body floats upon.
   Stated as a comparative: if body A is heavier than body B (same shape, same medium), A descends faster than B. If body A is heavier (per equal volume) than medium M, A sinks in M; if lighter, A floats on M.
   **Scope:** natural (unforced) downward or upward motion through a medium; comparisons only between bodies of similar shape, or between a body and its medium.
   **Preserved:** the weight of the body, and thus its ranking relative to other bodies and media, is preserved during the motion. Speed is not preserved (the arrow and the falling stone both change speed).
   **Boundary:** gives a ranking, not a numerical ratio. Says nothing about whether two equally heavy bodies of different shape descend equally (rule 3 handles that).

3. **Rule of Medium Resistance (operational).**
   The medium opposes a body's passage. Resistance increases:
   - with the *thickness* of the medium (honey > water > air),
   - with the *breadth of face* the body presents to the medium (flat sheet > compact ball, even at equal weight).
   For two bodies of equal weight, the one presenting the broader face arrives later. For one body in two media, it crosses the thinner medium sooner. A body so light that the medium's own motion (a breath of wind) exceeds its downward tendency will be carried sideways rather than fall straight.
   **Scope:** any body moving through any medium, whether by natural tendency or by impressed push.
   **Preserved:** the thickness of the medium and the body's shape are preserved during a given passage. The body's speed is not.
   **Boundary:** does not quantify the trade-off between weight and broadness of face; only ranks.

4. **Rule of Forced Motion Requiring a Pusher (operational).**
   A body moving along the ground, or otherwise *away from or across* its natural place rather than toward it, continues only while something pushes or pulls it. When the push ceases, the surrounding resistance (ground beneath, medium around) brings it to rest. Heavier loads press harder against the ground and so meet greater resistance: an ox must pull harder to keep a loaded wagon moving than an empty one, at the same pace.
   **Scope:** forced motion across the ground or otherwise not directed toward the body's natural place. Does not apply to natural fall or rise (rules 1–2), nor to flight after release (rule 5).
   **Preserved:** nothing about the motion is preserved once the push stops; the motion itself decays. The body's weight is preserved.
   **Boundary:** the link between weight and road-resistance is asserted but not independently observed.

5. **Rule of Impressed Push (operational).**
   When a pusher acts on a body and then releases it, a quantity of "impressed push" is left in the body. The body continues in the direction of the push until that impressed push is worn away by (a) the resistance of the medium it travels through and (b) its own tendency toward its natural place reasserting itself. Worn-away push cannot be recovered.
   Comparative consequences:
   - In a thicker medium, or against a broader face, the impressed push wears away faster.
   - On the ground (where resistance is great), it wears away very quickly (the cart of rule 4).
   - In open air (thin medium), it wears away slowly, so an arrow flies many paces before curving down.
   **Scope:** any body that has been pushed and then released, moving through a medium.
   **Preserved:** the impressed push is *not* conserved — it diminishes throughout the flight. What is preserved is the *direction* originally impressed, until the natural tendency overcomes the remaining push and bends the path.
   **Boundary:** does not quantify how much push is impressed by a given pusher, nor the rate at which it wears away beyond ranking by medium thickness.

6. **Rule of Celestial Motion (operational).**
   The Sun, Moon, and stars travel on fixed closed paths in the sky and return periodically to their previous positions. They do not fall toward the ground nor rise away from it; they neither speed up nor slow down on their paths within what the observations show.
   **Scope:** celestial bodies only (Sun, Moon, stars). Does not apply to anything that falls, rises, or is pushed on or near the ground.
   **Preserved:** the path itself and the period of return are preserved; the body returns to where it was.
   **Boundary:** the rule is descriptive, not explanatory. It is not derived from rules 1–5 and shares no mechanism with them.
