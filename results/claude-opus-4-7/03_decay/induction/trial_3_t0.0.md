# Proposed rules for this world

**Rule 1 — Universal multiplicative fade of motion.**
Any motion of any object — a swing, an oscillation, a fall, a slide, a spin, an orbit, a ringing vibration — steadily shrinks over time on its own, with nothing of the familiar kind needing to act on it. The fade is multiplicative: in equal elapsed times, motion retains an equal fraction of what it had. This gives the exponential curve shape seen in observations 2 and 9.

- Check on obs. 2: 10 cm → 3.7 cm in 100 s means the amplitude retains a fraction of about 1/e per 100 s; the shrink rate is roughly 1% per second.
- Check on obs. 9: 100 → 60.5 rad/s in 50 s is the same ≈1%-per-second multiplicative decline.
- This single rule covers obs. 2, 5, 6, 8, 9 directly: the spring weight, the bell's rim vibration, the sphere on the smooth track, the orbiting marble (its sideways motion fades, so its path tightens until contact), and the spinning top.

**Rule 2 — The fade rate depends on how slow the motion is.**
Slower swings, slower oscillations, slower falls lose a larger fraction per cycle (or per unit distance travelled) than faster ones, though the per-second fraction is similar. Equivalently, the shrink is set by elapsed time, not by how many swings fit into that time — so a long (slow) pendulum, which fits fewer swings into a given time, shows a bigger loss *per swing* than a short (fast) pendulum. This covers obs. 1.

**Rule 3 — Falling bodies approach a top speed.**
Under a steady downward pull, the fade of Rule 1 acts against the pull. The downward speed grows until the per-second shrink of Rule 1 just balances the per-second gain from the pull; beyond that point the speed does not increase, no matter how tall the track or how empty the chamber. This covers obs. 3, and also why the cannon shot in obs. 7 visibly slows in flight and lands short: its horizontal motion has no pull to replenish it and simply fades by Rule 1, while its vertical motion is capped by the top-speed limit.

**Rule 4 — The pull on a bob, and the fade of its swing, both scale with the bob's weight in the same ratio.**
Heavier bobs are pulled downward more, but their swing also fades in proportion, so two bobs of different weight (or different material) released together from the same angle trace the same angle at every later moment. This covers obs. 10. (It is consistent with Rule 1 if one reads the "motion" that fades as being measured per unit weight.)

**Rule 5 — Hot water cools on its own toward true zero, multiplicatively.**
Even with no contact, no air, and no radiation pathway out, the temperature of a body on the absolute scale shrinks by a fixed fraction per unit time, with nothing of the familiar kind carrying the heat away. From obs. 4: 353 K → 319 K in 10 s is a retention of about 0.9038 per 10 s, i.e. roughly 1% per second — the same characteristic shrink rate as Rules 1–2. The cooling rate is a property of the cup alone and does not depend on neighbours, exactly as reported.

This is the same kind of rule as Rule 1, applied to temperature on the absolute scale rather than to amplitude or speed. One could regard Rules 1 and 5 as a single principle: *any positive quantity describing the state of a body — swing amplitude, speed, spin rate, absolute temperature — fades by a roughly fixed fraction per second on its own.*

# Tensions and difficulties

The difference is that the falling body has a steady pull replenishing its downward motion, while the sealed cup has no comparable steady push warming it. So this is a difference in setup, not a contradiction — but it does mean Rule 1 and Rule 5 are only fully unified if we accept that temperature has no analogue of the downward pull.

- **Obs. 10 (equal-angle pendulums) vs. Rule 2.** Rule 2 says the per-swing fade depends on how slow the swing is. Two pendulums of the same length swing at the same rate regardless of bob weight, so Rule 2 alone predicts equal angles — but only if the per-second fade is also independent of bob weight and material. The observation that brass, glass, and ice bobs of equal weight all match supports this, and Rule 4 makes it explicit, but it is an extra assumption rather than a consequence of Rules 1–3.

- **Obs. 8 (tightening orbit).** Rule 1 predicts the sideways motion fades, which should tighten the path — good. But a strictly multiplicative fade of sideways speed, combined with a steady inward pull, gives a specific spiral shape; whether it matches "almost circular, slowly tightening" depends on the pull being strong compared to the per-second fade. The observations are consistent with this but do not pin it down.
