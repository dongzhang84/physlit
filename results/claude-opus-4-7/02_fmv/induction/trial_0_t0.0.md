# Rules of this world

1. **A push or pull produces a pace, not a change of pace.** Whenever a steady effort is applied to an object, that object immediately moves at one fixed pace for as long as the effort lasts. The pace appears the instant the effort begins and vanishes the instant the effort ends. There is no gradual speeding-up and no drifting onward after release.

2. **Pace is set by effort divided by heaviness.** For a given object, doubling or tripling the effort doubles or triples the pace. For a given effort, an object twice as heavy moves at half the pace. In short: pace = effort ÷ heaviness.

3. **No effort, no motion.** An object that is touched by no push or pull simply stays where it is. To carry a load anywhere, one must push it the entire way; stopping the push stops the load on the spot.

4. **Efforts in the same direction add; efforts in opposite directions subtract.** Two carriers pushing the same block the same way produce a pace equal to the sum of the paces each would produce alone. Two carriers pushing opposite ways produce a pace set by the difference of their efforts, in the direction of the stronger push. Equal opposing efforts leave the block at rest.

5. **These rules apply everywhere.** The track plays no role. An object adrift in open space behaves the same as one on a level track: steady push, steady pace; release, halt.

6. **Falling is a motion produced by a steady downward pull tied to the falling object itself.** Every object, while unsupported, is subject to a downward pull whose strength is exactly proportional to its own heaviness. By rule 2, the resulting pace (pull ÷ heaviness) is therefore the same for every object — a boulder and a pebble fall side by side at one fixed pace, and that pace does not grow as the fall continues.

7. **The downward pull acts through empty surroundings as well as through air.** Removing the air from a tall jar does not alter the fall; objects descend at the same single pace either way. The air is not what carries the pull, and it is not what holds objects back.

8. **A carried object shares the carrier's push only while contact lasts.** While the hand holds the stone and moves it, the hand's effort sets the stone's pace. The instant the stone is let go, no effort acts on it sideways, so its sideways motion ceases at once; only the downward pull of rule 6 remains, and the stone drops straight down from the point of release.

# Tensions

- **Observation 8 versus observation 2.** Rule 2 says one carrier's effort on a block of fixed heaviness gives one fixed pace. If two carriers push side by side, the total effort is doubled, so by rule 2 the pace should double — which matches observation 8 only when the two carriers happen to push with equal effort. The observation as worded ("the sum of the paces each produces alone") is consistent with rule 2 only because each carrier alone would produce half of the combined pace; stated more generally, the additivity in observation 8 and the proportionality in observation 4 fit together cleanly, but only under the reading that each carrier contributes their own effort to one shared total. This is consistent, but it does lean on a specific reading of "the pace each produces alone."

- **Observation 11 in detail.** Rule 8 explains why the stone drops straight down, but it requires that the hand's sideways push transmits no lingering motion at all — not even briefly. This is consistent with rule 1 (no push, no pace), yet it is worth flagging that the carried stone must somehow be "sharing" the hand's pace during carriage without retaining any trace of it after release. The rules permit this, but it is the most delicate point in the scheme.
