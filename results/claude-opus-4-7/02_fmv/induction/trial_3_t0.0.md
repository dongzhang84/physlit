# Rules of this world

1. **Pace follows push, not change of push.** At any moment, how fast a thing moves is fixed by the push or pull acting on it at that very moment. When the push is steady, the pace is steady; when the push stops, the pace stops.

2. **No carry-over of motion.** A thing has no tendency of its own to keep going. Nothing it did a moment ago is stored up in it. The present push alone sets the present pace; with no push, there is no motion.

3. **Pace begins and ends at once.** Because the present push alone sets the pace, starting a push produces the full pace immediately, and ending the push halts the thing immediately. There is no gradual speeding-up and no drifting-on.

4. **Pace grows in step with effort.** Double the effort gives double the pace, triple gives triple, and so on, in a simple proportion.

5. **Heaviness resists pace.** For the same effort, a thing twice as heavy glides at half the pace; three times as heavy, a third the pace. The pace produced by an effort is the effort divided by the heaviness.

6. **The track plays no part.** The same rules hold in open space, touching nothing, as on a long level track. There is no special slowing from the surface and no special help from it either.

7. **Pushes in the same line add and subtract.** Two pushes the same way give a pace equal to the sum of the paces each would give alone. Two pushes opposing each other give a pace set by the difference, in the direction of the stronger push; equal opposing pushes leave the thing where it is.

8. **Falling is a steady downward pull.** Whatever pulls things downward acts as a steady pull whose strength scales with heaviness in just the right way to give every falling thing the same one steady downward pace. Boulder and pebble fall together, and the pace of the fall does not grow as the fall continues.

9. **The downward pull does not depend on air.** It acts the same through open air and through a jar emptied of air.

10. **A thing carried shares only the carrier's present push.** While the hand moves the stone forward, the hand's forward push sets the stone's forward pace. The instant the hand lets go, that forward push is gone; only the downward pull remains, and the stone drops straight down from the point of release.

11. **To move a load, keep pushing.** Since nothing is carried over from earlier pushes, a load reaches its destination only if the carrier pushes the whole way.

# Tension

This is consistent but unexplained — it is an added stipulation about the downward pull rather than something that follows from the other rules.
