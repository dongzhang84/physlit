# Rules of this world

1. **An object moves only while something pushes or pulls it.** The moment the push or pull ends, the object halts where it is. Nothing carries onward on its own.

2. **A push or pull produces its full steady pace immediately.** There is no stretch of speeding-up from rest: the instant effort is applied, the object is already gliding at the pace that effort produces.

3. **The pace produced by a steady effort is exactly proportional to that effort.** Double the effort gives double the pace; triple the effort gives triple the pace; no effort gives no motion.

4. **The pace produced by a given effort is smaller for heavier objects, in exact inverse proportion to the heaviness.** A block twice as heavy glides at half the pace under the same pull. In short: pace = effort ÷ heaviness.

5. **This rule of motion holds everywhere** — on a track, in open space, in air, or in a jar emptied of air. The surroundings (track, air) do not enter into it. An object touching nothing behaves the same as one resting on a surface.

6. **Pushes and pulls applied to the same object combine by simple addition along their line of action.** Two efforts in the same direction give a pace equal to the sum of the paces each would produce alone. Two efforts in opposite directions give a pace set by their difference, in the direction of the larger effort; equal opposing efforts leave the object at rest.

7. **Falling is a steady downward pull of a particular fixed strength that acts on every object in proportion to its heaviness.** Because the downward pull on an object grows in step with its heaviness, and because heaviness divides into effort to set the pace (rule 4), every object falls at the same single steady pace, no matter how heavy or light, and that pace does not grow as the fall continues. The air, or its absence, plays no part.

8. **Being carried does not lend any motion to a thing.** A stone held in a moving hand moves only because the hand presses it along. The moment the hand lets go, no push or pull remains on the stone except the downward pull of falling, so it drops straight down from the point of release.

9. **To move a load from here to there, one must keep pushing the whole way**, since the load neither gathers pace nor coasts. Stopping the push stops the load on the spot.
