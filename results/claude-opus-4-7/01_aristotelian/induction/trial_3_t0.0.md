## Response

```
# Proposed Rules for This World

**1. Every body has a natural place toward which it tends to move on its own accord.**
Heavy things (stone, iron, water) belong below, at or beneath the earth's surface. Light things (smoke, flame) belong above, in the sky. When nothing holds a body away from its natural place, it begins to travel toward it.

**2. The strength of a body's striving toward its natural place is shown by its weight.**
A heavy stone strives strongly downward and so sinks in water (Obs. 5) and falls straight (Obs. 12). Smoke and flame have an upward striving (Obs. 3, 8) — even when the candle is tipped, the flame still seeks above, because its striving is toward the sky, not away from the wax. Rain-water strives downward and gathers on the ground (Obs. 4).

**3. The medium through which a body travels resists its passage, and this resistance is greater for thinner or more spread-out shapes.**
Honey resists more than air, so the pebble takes longer through honey than through air (Obs. 9). A hammered sheet, presenting a wide face, is resisted more by the air than the same metal balled up compactly, so the ball arrives first (Obs. 11). The flat pea is resisted more than the dense iron ball (Obs. 1). Feather-down is so light that even the gentlest stirring of air overwhelms its downward striving (Obs. 12).

**4. A body's downward progress through a resisting medium depends on both its striving (weight) and the medium's resistance.**
Greater weight overcomes resistance more readily; thus heavier bodies of similar shape descend faster than lighter ones (Obs. 1). A body whose downward striving is weaker than the upward push of the medium it sits in will not sink at all — dry oak on water remains afloat (Obs. 5).

**5. To keep a worldly body moving along the ground, something must continually push or pull it.**
Once the pusher's hands leave the cart, nothing sustains its rolling, and the road's roughness brings it to rest (Obs. 2). A loaded wagon presses harder against the road than an empty one and so demands more straining of the ox (Obs. 6).

**6. A body that has been violently set into motion through the air carries that motion onward for a time, the air itself helping to bear it forward, until the carrying motion fades and the body's natural downward striving takes over.**
The arrow flies straight for many paces, then curves down to the earth (Obs. 7). The carrying does not last forever; the downward striving was present all along but only governs the path once the forward carrying weakens.

**7. The heavenly bodies — Sun, Moon, fixed stars — are not of the same nature as earthly bodies.**
Their natural motion is neither downward nor upward but circular, and they repeat their daily round without slowing or falling (Obs. 10). The rules of striving toward earth or sky do not apply to them.
```
