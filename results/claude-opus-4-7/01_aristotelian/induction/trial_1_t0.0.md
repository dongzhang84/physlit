# Proposed Rules of This World

**1. Each thing has a natural place toward which it tends to travel when nothing holds it back.**
- Heavy things (iron, stone, pebbles, water) belong *down*, on or in the earth.
- Light things (smoke, flame) belong *up*, above the earth.
- This explains observations 3, 4, 5 (stone), 8 (flame always seeks upward regardless of candle orientation), and 12 (iron bar).

**2. The strength of a thing's downward tendency is shown by its weight, and heavier things press downward more strongly than lighter ones.**
- A loaded wagon presses the road harder than an empty one, so the ox must strain more to drag it (observation 6).

**3. The air resists the passage of things moving through it. This resistance is greater on broad or spread-out shapes and smaller on compact ones.**
- A flat sheet of metal meets more air than a ball of the same weight, so the ball arrives first (observation 11).
- A pea, being slight, is held back by the air more than the iron ball, which has so much downward tendency that the air barely slows it (observation 1).
- Feather-down is so slight that the air's stirrings push it about more strongly than its own faint downward tendency (observation 12).

**4. The thicker the surrounding stuff, the more strongly it resists passage through it.**
- Honey is far thicker than air, so a pebble creeps through honey but rushes through air (observation 9).

**5. A thing rises, sinks, or stays put in a body of water (or other stuff) according to whether its downward tendency is stronger or weaker than that of an equal bulk of the surrounding stuff.**
- Stone overcomes water and sinks; oak does not, and rests at the surface (observation 5).
- Smoke is lighter than air, so it rises through air until it reaches its like (observation 3).

**6. A thing at rest on the ground stays at rest unless something pushes or pulls it. A thing that is being pushed moves while the push lasts; once the push ends, the air and the ground take the motion out of it and it returns to rest.**
- The cart rolls while pushed and slows once released (observation 2).

**7. A push given to a thing can linger in it for a while after the pusher is gone, carrying it onward until the lingering push is spent; then the thing's downward tendency takes over.**
- The bowstring's push lingers in the arrow, carrying it forward through the air; as the push fades, the arrow's downward tendency bends its path earthward (observation 7).
- (This also fits observation 2: the cart's lingering push is quickly drained by the rough road.)

**8. The Sun, Moon, and stars are not earthly things and do not share the downward tendency. They belong to the sky and travel in circles there, returning each day.** (observation 10)

**9. A rough or yielding road draws the lingering push out of a rolling thing faster than a smooth one would.** (Supports observation 2; consistent with 6, since a heavier wagon presses the road harder and so is held back more strongly.)

# Tensions
