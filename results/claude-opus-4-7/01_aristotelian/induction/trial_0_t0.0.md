# Proposed Rules of This World

**1. Every body has a natural place toward which it tends to move when nothing holds it back.**
Heavy things (iron, stone, water, pebbles) have their natural place at or below the ground. Light things (smoke, flame) have their natural place above, in the open sky. When released, each body moves toward its own natural place. (Obs. 3, 4, 5, 8, 10 — heavenly bodies follow their own circular natural paths.)

**2. The strength of a body's tendency toward its natural place is greater the heavier the body is.**
Two bodies of the same shape but different weight do not descend equally well — the heavier presses through the surrounding air more strongly and arrives sooner. (Obs. 1, 12.)

**3. Every body that moves through a medium (air, water, honey) is resisted by that medium.**
The thicker the medium, the stronger the resistance: a pebble drops quickly through air, slowly through honey. (Obs. 9.)

**4. Resistance from the medium also depends on the shape and spread of the body.**
A body spread out across a wide surface is held up by more of the medium than the same body gathered into a compact lump. Hence a thin sheet of metal descends more slowly than a ball of equal weight, and a pea or feather-down — having little weight to push aside much air — descends slowly or drifts. (Obs. 1, 11, 12.)

**5. A breeze (moving air) can push a light body sideways.**
Where the body's downward tendency is weak, even a slight current of air carries it. Where the downward tendency is strong (iron bar), the air's push has no visible effect. (Obs. 12.)

**6. Whether a body sinks or floats in a liquid depends on the kinds of stuff involved, not on weight alone.**
Stone belongs below water and sinks through it; oak belongs above water and rests on its surface. Each material has its own proper level relative to others. (Obs. 5; consistent with 3, 4.)

**7. A body at rest on the ground stays at rest unless something pushes or pulls it; a body that is being pushed or pulled along the ground keeps moving only while the pushing or pulling continues.**
Once the push stops, the ground and air work against the body and bring it to rest in a short distance. (Obs. 2.)

**8. The harder a body presses toward its natural place (i.e., the heavier it is), the more strongly the ground resists being slid against, and the harder one must strain to drag it.**
A loaded wagon presses the road more than an empty one, so the ox must pull harder. (Obs. 6.)

**9. A body that has been set moving through the air carries its motion onward for a while after the pusher releases it, but the air gradually wears the motion away, and meanwhile the body's downward tendency is always at work.**
An arrow flies forward many paces, but at every moment the air saps its forward motion and the earthward tendency adds to its descent, so its path eventually curves down to the ground. (Obs. 7; also consistent with the cart in Obs. 2, where ground resistance is much stronger than air's.)

**10. Flame is a body whose natural place is upward, and it seeks that place from wherever its source happens to lie.**
Tilting the candle does not tilt the flame; the flame still rises away from the wax toward the sky. (Obs. 8.)

**11. The Sun, Moon, and stars are bodies whose natural motion is circular and unending, unlike earthly bodies whose natural motion is straight toward ground or sky.**
They neither fall nor rise to rest; they simply continue their daily round. (Obs. 10.)

## Tensions

The observations give no way to derive *why* their natural motion is circular and unceasing while earthly natural motion is straight and terminates; this split is stipulated rather than explained.
