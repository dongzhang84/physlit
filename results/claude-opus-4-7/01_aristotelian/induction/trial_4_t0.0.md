# Proposed Rules of This World

**1. Every body has a natural place toward which it tends to move when nothing holds it back.**
Heavy things (stone, iron, water) belong below, on or in the earth. Light things (smoke, flame) belong above, in the open sky. When released, a body travels toward its proper place.

**2. The "heaviness" of a body is shown by its weight and by how strongly it seeks the earth.**
A stone sinks through water (obs. 5); rain collects on the ground (obs. 4); an iron bar drops straight (obs. 12). Wood, being less heavy than water, rests on the surface rather than descending through it (obs. 5).

**3. The "lightness" of a body is shown by its tendency to rise.**
Smoke rises and disperses upward (obs. 3); flame always points upward away from its source, regardless of how the candle is held (obs. 8). Up-seeking is a real tendency, not merely the absence of down-seeking.

**4. The medium a body travels through resists its passage, and the resistance is greater for thicker (denser, stickier) media than for thin ones.**
A pebble settles slowly through honey but quickly through air (obs. 9). Air itself offers some resistance, just much less than honey.

**5. For bodies of comparable shape, a heavier body overcomes the medium's resistance more readily and so reaches its natural place sooner.**
The iron ball arrives before the pea (obs. 1) because its greater downward tendency more easily parts the air.

**6. For bodies of equal heaviness, a compact shape parts the medium more easily than a spread-out shape.**
The metal ball lands before the hammered sheet (obs. 11). The wisp of down, being both very light and very spread out, is barely able to descend at all and is pushed about by any motion in the air (obs. 12).

**7. A body at rest on the earth stays at rest unless something moves it; a body in motion along the earth keeps moving only so long as something keeps moving it.**
The cart rolls while pushed and stops once released (obs. 2). The road and the cart together consume the motion that was given to it.

**8. Heavier loads consume motion more rapidly and require a stronger sustained pull to keep moving.**
The ox strains harder with a loaded wagon than an empty one (obs. 6).

**9. A body can be given motion in a direction other than its natural one, and it will continue in that imparted direction for a time, but the imparted motion fades while the natural downward tendency reasserts itself.**
The arrow flies forward many paces, then curves down to the earth as its forward motion is spent (obs. 7).

**10. The Sun, Moon, and fixed stars are not earthly bodies and do not share the tendencies of rules 1–3.**
They follow their own circular daily courses through the heavens and return to the same positions (obs. 10). The heavens above and the earthly region below obey different orders.

# Tensions

- **Observation 7 (the arrow)** is the most strained. Rule 9 asserts that imparted motion "fades," but the rules do not explain *why* motion through still air should fade when the air is also what (per rule 4) the arrow is parting. One might expect the parted air to keep pushing the arrow along, or alternatively to stop it almost at once; the gradual curving descent sits awkwardly between these.
- **Observation 2 (the cart)** has a related difficulty: the cart stops "within a short distance," but rule 7 does not specify what consumes the motion (the road, the air, or the cart itself), only that it is consumed. The arrow and the cart may need a more unified account of why imparted motion lasts longer in some cases than others.
- **Observation 10 (heavenly bodies)** is handled by exempting the heavens from the earthly rules rather than by deriving their behavior — a real gap, not a contradiction.
