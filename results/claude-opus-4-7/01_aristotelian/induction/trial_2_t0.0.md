# Proposed Rules of This World

**1. Each thing has a natural place toward which it tends to travel when nothing holds it back.**
- Heavy things (iron, stone, pebbles, water) belong *down*, at or under the earth's surface.
- Light things (smoke, flame) belong *up*, away from the earth.
- Some things (oak wood, feather-down) belong somewhere in between, near the surface of water or drifting in air.

**2. The strength of a thing's tendency toward its natural place depends on what kind of thing it is and how much of it is gathered together.** Iron tends downward more strongly than dried pea-stuff; stone more strongly than oak. This tendency is what we perceive as *weight*.

**3. A thing moves toward its natural place by traveling through whatever medium lies in the way (air, water, honey, etc.). The medium resists being parted.** The thicker the medium, the slower the passage. Hence:
- A pebble passes quickly through air but slowly through honey (obs. 9).
- Water released from a cloud passes down through air and gathers on the ground (obs. 4).

**4. The resistance a medium offers to a passing thing depends on how much of the thing's surface meets the medium, not only on its weight.** Therefore:
- A flat hammered sheet is held back by the air more than a compact ball of equal weight (obs. 11).
- A dried pea, being light and broad relative to its substance, is held back more than an iron ball (obs. 1).
- Feather-down, presenting much surface for little weight, barely descends and is pushed about by stirrings of the air (obs. 12).

**5. Flame and smoke are of the upward-belonging kind.** They rise through air toward their proper region regardless of how their source is oriented — a tilted candle still sends its flame upward, because the flame seeks its place, not the candle's end (obs. 8). Smoke likewise rises and disperses high above (obs. 3).

**6. A thing placed in a medium denser than itself is held up by that medium and rests at the boundary.** Oak in water rests at the surface; stone, being of the heavier kind, parts the water and continues to the bottom (obs. 5).

**7. A thing at rest on the ground stays at rest unless something pushes or pulls it. A thing being pushed moves only while the pushing continues; once the push ceases, the medium and the ground drag it back to rest.** Hence the cart rolls while pushed and slows to a stop when released (obs. 2).

**8. To keep a heavier load moving requires a stronger and more sustained pull, because the ground and the load's own downward tendency resist motion more.** A loaded wagon strains the ox more than an empty one (obs. 6).

**9. Thus an arrow flies far and straight before arcing down (obs. 7).

**10. The heavenly bodies — Sun, Moon, fixed stars — are not of the same kind as earthly things. They neither fall nor rise but travel in circles overhead, returning each day to where they were** (obs. 10). Their nature is to circle, as the nature of stones is to descend and of flame to ascend.

## Tensions and Difficulties

- **Observation 7 (the arrow)** is the most awkward. Rule 7 says things stop moving once the pusher stops pushing — yet the arrow flies on after leaving the string. Rule 9 patches this with a "carried-push" that lingers in the thing itself, but this is uncomfortably close to making an exception: why does the cart not also coast on a carried-push when released? One might say the bowstring imparts a much sharper, more concentrated push than a hand on a cart, or that the cart's contact with the ground draws the push out of it quickly while the arrow only touches air. The rules accommodate the observation but do not deeply explain why the two cases differ.

- **Observation 10 (the heavens)** is handled by simply declaring heavenly bodies a separate kind. This is consistent but unsatisfying — it is a stipulation rather than an explanation, and it leaves unanswered why some things belong down, some up, and some in eternal circles.
