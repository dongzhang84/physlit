### Stage 2 — formulation response (under judgment, 6,902 chars)

```
# Operational Rules

**1. Natural region and tendency of motion.**
- *Operational form:* Every body is of one of two kinds — heavy (stone, iron, water, wood, lead, wheat, cloth, leaf, feather) or light (smoke, flame). A heavy body, when not held or pushed, moves toward the earth along the straight line that leads most directly to it (downward). A light body, when not held or pushed, moves away from the earth along the same line in the opposite sense (upward). The orientation of the body's source does not change which direction it tends.
- *Scope:* Terrestrial bodies only. Does not apply to Sun, Moon, stars (see Rule 6). Does not apply while a body is being actively pushed or carried, though the tendency persists and resumes when the push stops.
- *Preserved:* The *direction* of the tendency (toward or away from earth) is preserved for a given body throughout its existence. The body's kind (heavy or light) is preserved. Speed of approach to the natural region is not preserved — it depends on weight and medium (Rules 2, 3).
- *Boundary cases:* The rule does not say *how far* a light body rises before stopping, nor what determines where smoke disperses. The classification heavy/light is given by enumeration, not derived.

**2. Eagerness versus resistance of the medium.**
- *Operational form:* For two heavy bodies of the same shape and size in the same medium, the heavier reaches the earth sooner. For one heavy body in two different media, it reaches the earth sooner in the thinner medium (air sooner than water sooner than honey). If a body's weight is less than the upward resistance the medium offers to its lower surface, the body does not descend at all but rests at the medium's surface (oak on water). Ranking, not numerical ratio, is what the rule fixes.
- *Scope:* Heavy bodies in fluid media (air, water, honey). Does not govern light bodies' ascent rates. Does not govern bodies resting on solid ground (Rule 4).
- *Preserved:* The ranking — denser-medium-slower, heavier-body-faster — holds throughout the descent. No numerical quantity is conserved.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule gives only comparative speeds, not a speed for a single body in isolation. It does not say whether a descending body speeds up, slows down, or holds steady as it falls; the observations did not distinguish these.

**3. Shape and surface against the medium.**
- *Operational form:* For two bodies of equal weight in the same medium, the one whose surface presented to the direction of travel is larger descends more slowly. When weight is very small and presented surface is very large (feather-down), the medium's own stirrings (wind, drafts) can move the body sideways or upward by more than its descent in still medium, so its path is not straight downward. A flat sheet of given material descends more slowly than a crumpled ball of the same material and weight.
- *Scope:* Heavy bodies descending through a fluid medium. Applies in combination with Rule 2.
- *Preserved:* For a body of fixed shape and weight in a fixed medium, the qualitative manner of fall (straight and steady, or wandering) is preserved. If the shape changes (sheet crumpled into ball), the descent changes accordingly.
- *Boundary cases:* "Stirrings of the medium" are invoked but not characterized; the rule cannot predict *where* a feather will land, only that its path is irregular.

**4. Rest, push, and continuance.**
- *Operational form:* A body resting on the ground does not move unless pushed or pulled. While the push or pull continues, the body moves; when the push or pull stops, the body stops within a short distance. A heavier load requires harder pushing or pulling to move at the same pace; the same effort moves a heavier load more slowly or not at all.
- *Scope:* Terrestrial bodies in contact with the ground (carts, sledges, loads). Does not apply to projectiles once released (see Rule 5), nor to natural descent or ascent (Rules 1–3).
- *Preserved:* While the push is steady and the load and road unchanged, the pace of motion is preserved. When the push ceases, motion ceases.
- *Boundary cases:* "Short distance" after the push stops is not quantified. The rule does not say how much harder one must pull for a doubled load — only that more is required.

**5. Projectile travel.**
- *Operational form:* A body launched into the air by a momentary push (arrow from string, stone from hand) continues in the direction it was sent. As it travels, two things happen together: its forward travel weakens with distance covered, and its heavy nature (Rule 1) bends its path downward. It strikes the earth when the downward bending brings it there. A more vigorous launch sends it farther before it lands.
- *Scope:* Heavy bodies launched into air with a single initial push, then released. Does not apply to bodies under continuous push (Rule 4) or to light bodies.
- *Preserved:* The initial direction of motion is preserved in tendency, though increasingly overridden by the downward bending. Nothing is preserved in quantity; both forward travel and height above ground change throughout the flight.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule does not specify how forward travel weakens with distance, nor at what rate the downward bending takes over. It cannot predict the landing point numerically. The tension with Rule 4 (why the arrow continues but the cart does not) is named but not resolved.

**6. Celestial motion.**
- *Operational form:* Sun, Moon, and stars move along fixed circular tracks in the sky. Each completes its circuit in a fixed period (Sun and stars daily; Moon nearly so). Their motion neither speeds up, slows down, nor strays from its track.
- *Scope:* Celestial bodies only. Rules 1–5 do not apply to them, nor does this rule apply to anything terrestrial.
- *Preserved:* The track, the direction along the track, and the period of circuit are all preserved.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule is stipulated, not derived. It gives no account of why these bodies behave differently from terrestrial ones, and no way to predict eclipses, planetary wandering, or comets — only the regular daily and monthly circuits noted in the observations.

# Boundary notes

The operational rules give only rankings and qualitative trajectories, not numerical predictions: I cannot say *how many paces* an arrow flies, *how many counts* a stone takes to reach the bottom of a pond, or *how much harder* a doubled load must be pulled. The arrow-versus-cart tension (Rules 4 vs. 5) remains a stipulated difference. The heavy/light classification underlying Rule 1 is given by enumeration of cases rather than by any deeper criterion, so a new substance (say, oil, or a hot vapor) cannot be classified without further observation. The feather's wandering path (Rule 3) is acknowledged but not predictable. Celestial motion (Rule 6) is wholly separate from terrestrial motion with no bridge between them.

# Predictions

**Scenario 1. Iron ball vs. hollow wooden ball, both 30 m in still air.**

Both balls are heavy bodies (iron and wood appear in the enumeration of Rule 1), and both descend toward the earth. They have the same outer shape and size, so by Rule 3 the surface presented to the direction of travel is the same. By Rule 2, when two heavy bodies of the same shape and size fall through the same medium, the heavier reaches the earth sooner. The solid iron ball is much heavier than the hollow wooden ball, so the iron ball strikes the ground first, and the wooden ball arrives noticeably later.

As to *how much* sooner — my rules fix only the ranking, not a numerical ratio (see the boundary note on Rule 2 and the general boundary note). I cannot give a count of seconds or a ratio of times. I can say only that the gap is appreciable because the difference in weight is large.

*Rules used: 1, 2, 3.*

---

**Scenario 2. Cart given one push on perfectly smooth ice.**

This scenario sits in a gap between my rules. Rule 4 covers carts on the ground: while pushed they move, and when the push stops they stop within a short distance. Rule 5 covers projectiles launched into the air by a momentary push: they continue in the sent direction while their forward travel weakens, and their heavy nature bends them down to the earth. The cart on ice is neither quite a normal cart on a road (the ice is described as perfectly smooth, unlike the ordinary roads my Rule 4 was drawn from) nor a projectile in the air.

My rules name but do not resolve the tension between Rule 4 and Rule 5: why an arrow continues after release but a cart does not. They give me no principle for deciding which regime "perfectly smooth ice" belongs to. If I treat the cart as a body on the ground, Rule 4 says it stops within a short distance after the push ceases. If I treat the smoothness of the ice as making it more like a projectile that has been released, Rule 5 would have its forward travel weaken with distance — but Rule 5 explicitly concerns bodies launched *into the air*, and the cart remains in contact with the ice.

So I must say plainly: this case is outside the clean scope of my rules. The nearest rule (Rule 4) would predict the cart stops within a short distance, but I have no warrant for asserting that "short distance" still applies when the surface is unlike any in the observations behind Rule 4.

*Rules consulted: 4, 5; neither cleanly applies.*

---

**Scenario 3. Two stones, A twice the weight of B, released just below the surface of a pond.**

Both are heavy bodies of identical shape and size in the same medium (water). By Rule 2, the heavier reaches the earth (here, the pond bottom) sooner; so stone A reaches the bottom first.

The ratio of times is not something my rules can supply. Rule 2 explicitly fixes ranking, not numerical ratio (its boundary note: "Ranking, not numerical ratio, is what the rule fixes"). I cannot say whether A arrives in half B's time, or in some other proportion. I can say only that A arrives first and B second.

*Rules used: 1, 2.*

---

**Scenario 4. Feather released in a sealed chamber with nothing inside.**

This scenario lies outside the scope of my rules. Rule 1 says a heavy body, when not held or pushed, moves toward the earth, and a feather is enumerated as heavy. But Rules 2 and 3 — which govern the *manner* of descent — are explicitly framed for descent *through a fluid medium* (air, water, honey), and Rule 3 in particular makes the feather's behavior depend on the medium's resistance to its large presented surface and on the medium's stirrings. With nothing in the chamber, there is no medium.

My rules do not tell me what happens to a heavy body when there is no medium at all. I will not silently import a standard-physics answer (such as that the feather falls just like the iron ball). I note only that Rule 1's tendency would still point the feather earthward, but the *manner and rate* of its descent are not determined by my rules in this case.

*Rules consulted: 1, 2, 3; Rules 2 and 3 cannot be applied because their scope requires a medium.*

**Scenario 5. Arrow fired horizontally; what sustains its forward motion, and why does it fall?**

My rule does not name an agent that "sustains" the forward motion; it simply states that the initial direction of motion is preserved as a tendency after release, while weakening with distance covered.
