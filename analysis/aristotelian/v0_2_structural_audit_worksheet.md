# Proposed Rules for This World

**1. Every body has a natural place toward which it tends to move on its own accord.**
Heavy things (stone, iron, water) belong below, at or beneath the earth's surface. Light things (smoke, flame) belong above, in the sky. When nothing holds a body away from its natural place, it begins to travel toward it.

**2. The strength of a body's striving toward its natural place is shown by its weight.**
A heavy stone strives strongly downward and so sinks in water (Obs. 5) and falls straight (Obs. 12). Smoke and flame have an upward striving (Obs. 3, 8) — even when the candle is tipped, the flame still seeks above, because its striving is toward the sky, not away from the wax. Rain-water strives downward and gathers on the ground (Obs. 4).

**3. The medium through which a body travels resists its passage, and this resistance is greater for thinner or more spread-out shapes.**
Honey resists more than air, so the pebble takes longer through honey than through air (Obs. 9). A hammered sheet, presenting a wide face, is resisted more by the air than the same metal balled up compactly, so the ball arrives first (Obs. 11). The flat pea is resisted more than the dense iron ball (Obs. 1). Feather-down is so light that even the gentlest stirring of air overwhelms its downward striving (Obs. 12).

**4. A body's downward progress through a resisting medium depends on both its striving (weight) and the medium's resistance.**
Greater weight overcomes resistance more readily; thus heavier bodies of similar shape descend faster than lighter ones (Obs. 1). A body whose downward striving is weaker than the upward push of the medium it sits in will not sink at all — dry oak on water remains afloat (Obs. 5).

**5. To keep a worldly body moving along the ground, something must continually push or pull it.**
Once the pusher's hands leave the cart, nothing sustains its rolling, and the road's roughness brings it to rest (Obs. 2). A loaded wagon presses harder against the road than an empty one and so demands more straining of the ox (Obs. 6).

**6. A body that has been violently set into motion through the air carries that motion onward for a time, the air itself helping to bear it forward, until the carrying motion fades and the body's natural downward striving takes over.**
The arrow flies straight for many paces, then curves down to the earth (Obs. 7). The carrying does not last forever; the downward striving was present all along but only governs the path once the forward carrying weakens.

**7. The heavenly bodies — Sun, Moon, fixed stars — are not of the same nature as earthly bodies.**
Their natural motion is neither downward nor upward but circular, and they repeat their daily round without slowing or falling (Obs. 10). The rules of striving toward earth or sky do not apply to them.

## Observations in Tension

- **Obs. 7 (the arrow)** sits least comfortably. Rule 5 says a thing moving along the ground stops once nothing pushes it, yet Rule 6 grants the arrow continued flight with no hand on it. The difference invoked — that air "helps to bear" a violently launched body but does not help the cart — is a stipulation rather than something the observations directly demand. Why air sustains the arrow but slows the falling pea (Rule 3) is not fully reconciled; the air seems to play opposite roles in the two cases.
- **Obs. 10 (heavenly bodies)** is handled only by exempting them from the other rules. That is consistent but unexplanatory: it tells us the heavens are different without saying what governs their circular return.

# Operational Rules

**1. Natural place and spontaneous motion.**
- *Operational form:* Every body belongs to one of two terrestrial kinds: heavy (stone, iron, water, pea, oak-wood) or light (smoke, flame). A heavy body, when not held or supported, begins to travel along the straight line toward the earth's surface; a light body, when not enclosed, begins to travel along the straight line away from the earth's surface. Direction of natural motion depends only on the kind of body, not on its surroundings or orientation.
- *Scope:* Terrestrial bodies only. Applies whenever the body is not being pushed, pulled, held, or carried by a violent motion (Rule 6).
- *Preserved:* The body's kind (heavy or light) and therefore the direction of its natural striving are preserved throughout its motion. Position is not preserved; speed is not specified by this rule alone.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule does not say at what speed natural motion begins, nor whether a body just released begins slowly and quickens. Obs. 12 (feather drifting) is awkward: the feather is heavy in kind but does not visibly fall straight.

**2. Weight as the measure of striving.**
- *Operational form:* For two heavy bodies A and B of the same shape and size in the same medium, if A weighs more than B (as measured on a balance), then A descends more quickly than B. Ranking by weight gives ranking by speed of descent. For light bodies, the analogous rule applies upward, but the observations give no balance-measurement of lightness, so only ordinal comparison is available (flame rises, smoke rises).
- *Scope:* Heavy or light terrestrial bodies of comparable shape and size, in the same medium, undergoing natural motion.
- *Preserved:* Weight itself is preserved as the body moves (a stone weighs the same on the ground as in the air). The ranking among bodies by weight is preserved.
- *Boundary cases:* Bodies of differing shape or size cannot be compared by weight alone — Rule 3 must be used jointly. The rule gives no numerical proportionality, only ranking.

**3. Resistance of the medium.**
- *Operational form:* For a given body, descent is slower in a denser medium (honey) than in a thinner one (air); rank the media by observed slowness of a standard pebble passing through them. For a given quantity of matter (same weight), a shape that presents a wider face to the direction of motion is resisted more, and so descends more slowly, than a compact shape. Thus: hammered sheet slower than ball of the same metal (Obs. 11); flat pea slower than iron ball (Obs. 1).
- *Scope:* All terrestrial bodies moving through any medium (air, water, honey). Does not apply to celestial bodies.
- *Preserved:* The medium's resisting character is preserved (honey always resists more than air). The body's shape, if unchanged, gives a constant degree of resistance.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule does not quantify how much slower; it gives only rankings. The feather (Obs. 12) is an extreme case where the air's stirrings dominate the body's striving entirely.

**4. Joint dependence of descent on weight and resistance.**
- *Operational form:* Whether a heavy body descends at all in a medium is decided by comparison: place the body in the medium; if it sinks, its striving exceeds the medium's upward push (e.g., iron in water); if it floats, the medium's upward push exceeds the striving (dry oak on water, Obs. 5). When it does descend, speed increases with greater weight and decreases with greater resistance (combining Rules 2 and 3).
- *Scope:* Heavy bodies in fluid media (water, honey, air).
- *Preserved:* The outcome (sink or float) is preserved for a given body in a given medium — a piece of oak that floats once will float again. The body's weight is preserved.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule cannot predict speeds when weight and shape both differ between two bodies; it only resolves comparisons in which one variable is held fixed.

**5. Maintenance of motion along the ground.**
- *Operational form:* A body in contact with the ground moves only while something (hand, ox, slope) is in contact with it pushing or pulling. Remove the pusher and the body slows and stops. The straining required of the pusher is greater when the body presses harder on the road; loading a wagon increases the press and so increases the strain (Obs. 6).
- *Scope:* Terrestrial bodies traveling along a surface (cart on road, wagon on road). Does not apply to bodies in flight (Rule 6) or in free fall (Rules 1–4).
- *Preserved:* Nothing about the body's motion is preserved once the pusher releases it; the motion fades. The body's weight and the road's roughness are preserved.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule does not give a stopping-distance. It does not address smooth surfaces (ice, polished floor), which the observations do not include.

**6. Violent motion through the air.**
- *Operational form:* When a body is launched through the air by a violent push (bowstring, throw), it travels in the direction of the push for some distance, the air bearing it forward; during this carrying phase the path is approximately straight. The carrying weakens with distance traveled, and once weakened the body's downward striving (Rules 1–4) governs and curves the path to the ground (Obs. 7). Heavier launched bodies of the same shape carry farther under the same push than lighter ones; bodies presenting a wide face to the air carry less far than compact ones.
- *Scope:* Terrestrial bodies set in motion through air (or other thin medium) by an initial violent push, after the pusher has released them.
- *Preserved:* The direction of the original push is preserved during the carrying phase. Nothing is preserved quantitatively; the carrying simply fades.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule stipulates that air carries the arrow but resists the falling pea, without saying why air plays opposite roles. It gives no rule for distance carried as a function of launch strength. It does not address violent motion along the ground (a kicked stone), which the observations do not include.

**7. Celestial motion.**
- *Operational form:* The Sun, Moon, and fixed stars travel along closed circular paths and return to the same position after a fixed period (one day for the daily round, Obs. 10). Their speed along the circle does not slacken and they do not approach or recede from the earth.
- *Scope:* Heavenly bodies only. Rules 1–6 do not apply to them, and this rule does not apply to terrestrial bodies.
- *Preserved:* The shape of the path (a circle), the period of return, and the speed along the path are all preserved indefinitely.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule describes but does not explain the circular return. It does not say why the heavens differ in nature from earthly bodies, only that they do.

## Boundary notes

Several observations remain awkward under these operational rules. **Obs. 7 (the arrow)** still requires the unexplained stipulation that air carries violently launched bodies but resists falling ones — Rules 3 and 6 assign opposite roles to air without reconciling them. **Obs. 10 (heavenly bodies)** is handled only by exemption: Rule 7 records the circular return as a brute fact rather than deriving it. **Obs. 12 (the feather)** is partially handled by Rule 3 (high resistance against a wide light shape) but the rules give no way to predict the feather's drifting path, only that it falls slowly. **Obs. 5 (oak floating)** is handled qualitatively by Rule 4 but the rules give no way to predict, before the test, which woods will float and which will sink. None of the rules supplies numerical proportionalities; all comparisons are ordinal.

# Operational Rules

**1. Natural place and natural motion.**
- *Operational form:* Every terrestrial body has a natural place. Heavy bodies (stone, iron, water) have their natural place at or below the surface of the earth; light bodies (smoke, flame) have their natural place above, in the upper air. When unsupported and not pushed, a body travels in a straight line toward its natural place: heavy bodies downward, light bodies upward.
- *Scope:* Terrestrial bodies only. Applies whenever no support holds the body and no imparted motion is currently driving it elsewhere (and even then, the natural tendency is present concurrently — see rule 9).
- *Preserved:* The direction of natural tendency for a given body is preserved throughout its existence; it does not change with location on earth or with orientation of its container (flame still points up when the candle is tilted).
- *Boundary cases:* Wood in water (obs. 5) is "heavy" relative to air but "light" relative to water — natural place is medium-relative, which the rule accommodates only by ranking, not absolutely.

**2. Heaviness as ranking.**
- *Operational form:* Bodies can be ranked by heaviness. Body A is heavier than body B if, when both are placed in the same medium, A descends through B (or B rises through A). Examples of the ranking established by the observations: iron > stone > water > wood > air > smoke, flame.
- *Scope:* Terrestrial bodies in terrestrial media. The ranking is comparative, not numerical: the observations give no unit of heaviness.
- *Preserved:* A body's place in the heaviness ranking is preserved; it does not change with motion or location.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule does not say by how much one body is heavier than another, only the order. Cases where two bodies are very close in heaviness are not addressed.

**3. Lightness as a positive tendency.**
- *Operational form:* A body counted as "light" in rule 2's ranking (above the medium it is in) actively rises through that medium toward the upper region. Lightness is a directed tendency upward, not merely failure to descend.
- *Scope:* Terrestrial bodies less heavy than the surrounding medium.
- *Preserved:* The upward direction of motion, relative to the earth, regardless of the body's prior orientation or the orientation of any container.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule does not specify whether a very light body rises faster than a moderately light body rises, by analogy to rule 5; the observations do not test this.

**4. Resistance of the medium.**
- *Operational form:* Media can be ranked by thickness (resistance to passage): honey > water > air. For the same body, descent (or ascent) is slower through a thicker medium and faster through a thinner one.
- *Scope:* Any terrestrial body moving through any terrestrial medium, whether by natural tendency or by imparted motion.
- *Preserved:* The thickness ranking of the media themselves; it is a property of the medium, not of any body in it.
- *Boundary cases:* The observations give no quantitative relation between thicknesses, only ordering. Whether the same medium offers the same resistance to upward and downward passage is not specified.

**5. Heavier bodies arrive sooner (same shape).**
- *Operational form:* Given two bodies of comparable shape released together from the same height in the same medium, the heavier (per rule 2) reaches the earth first. The time-difference is greater when the medium is thicker.
- *Scope:* Natural downward motion of terrestrial bodies of similar shape in the same medium. Symmetric statement for upward motion of light bodies is not directly attested by the observations.
- *Preserved:* The order of arrival follows the heaviness ranking; this ordering does not change with the height of the drop.
- *Boundary cases:* "Comparable shape" is not made precise. Two bodies of equal heaviness but different shape are governed instead by rule 6.

**6. Compact shapes arrive sooner (same heaviness).**
- *Operational form:* Given two bodies of equal heaviness released together from the same height in the same medium, the more compact (less spread-out) one reaches the earth first. A body that is both very light and very spread out (down) may fail to descend at all and instead drift with motions in the air.
- *Scope:* Natural motion of terrestrial bodies through a resisting medium. The rule presumes the medium has nonzero thickness.
- *Preserved:* The shape-based ordering for given pairs of bodies in given media.
- *Boundary cases:* "Compact" versus "spread-out" is comparative and not made numerical. The down-wisp (obs. 12) is at the limit of the rule: the rule predicts that it descends, but only barely, and any breath of air dominates.

**7. Rest persists; imparted horizontal motion does not.**
- *Operational form:* A terrestrial body at rest on the earth remains at rest until something pushes or pulls it. A body moving along the earth in a direction other than its natural direction continues only while the push or pull is sustained; once the push ceases, the body comes to rest within a finite distance.
- *Scope:* Imparted (forced) motion of terrestrial bodies, particularly along the surface of the earth. Does not apply to natural up/down tendencies, which need no sustaining push.
- *Preserved:* Nothing quantitative is preserved during forced motion. The motion is "consumed" but no measure of it is conserved or tracked. Rest, once attained, is preserved.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule does not specify the stopping distance; this depends on heaviness (rule 8), shape, and medium in ways the observations do not pin down.

**8. Heavier loads consume motion faster.**
- *Operational form:* For a given sustained push or pull, a heavier load advances less far, or requires a stronger sustained pull to advance at the same rate, than a lighter load. Equivalently: when the push is removed, a heavier moving load comes to rest sooner than a lighter one would under otherwise similar conditions — but only as a comparative statement.
- *Scope:* Terrestrial bodies in forced motion along the earth (carts, wagons, sledges).
- *Preserved:* The heaviness ordering between two loads is preserved through the motion; the relative effort required to move them does not change with the duration of the pulling.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule does not handle obs. 2 (cart stopping) and obs. 7 (arrow stopping) in a unified way: it explains only that heavier things are harder to keep going, not why any forced motion fades at all.

**9. Imparted motion fades; natural tendency reasserts itself.**
- *Operational form:* A body given motion in a direction other than its natural one travels in that imparted direction for some span of distance and then ceases to do so. While the imparted motion persists, it adds to the natural tendency, producing a curved path: the imparted-direction component shrinks over time while the natural component (downward, for heavy bodies) does not. The body's path therefore bends increasingly toward its natural direction until only the natural motion remains.
- *Scope:* Terrestrial bodies in forced motion away from their natural place — projectiles, thrown stones, arrows.
- *Preserved:* The natural downward tendency is preserved throughout the flight (a heavy body never loses its heaviness). The imparted motion is not preserved; it diminishes and disappears.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule does not say at what rate the imparted motion fades, nor whether the medium itself causes the fading or merely accompanies it. Obs. 7 (the arrow) is therefore handled only qualitatively — the curved descent is described, not derived.

**10. Heavenly bodies follow a different order.**
- *Operational form:* The Sun, Moon, and fixed stars move along closed circular paths and return to the same configurations after a regular period (one day for the daily course). They do not fall toward the earth, do not rise away from it, and are not subject to rules 1–9.
- *Scope:* Celestial bodies only. The boundary between the celestial and terrestrial regions is not made precise by the observations.
- *Preserved:* The circular path of each heavenly body is preserved; the period of recurrence is preserved.
- *Boundary cases:* The rule is purely descriptive. It does not explain *why* heavenly bodies move circularly, nor what sustains their motion when terrestrial imparted motion fades. Obs. 10 is acknowledged rather than explained.

Finally, the symmetric case of light bodies — whether smoke rises faster than flame, or whether shape affects ascent the way it affects descent — is not addressed by any observation, and our rules 5 and 6 are stated only for downward natural motion.
