# Operational Rules

## Rule 1 — Motion lasts exactly as long as the push

1. **Operational statement.** At every instant, check whether any push or pull is acting on the body. If yes, the body is moving at that instant; if no, the body is stationary at that instant. There is no delay at the start of a push and no continuation after a push ends. The body's position at any moment is its starting position plus the running total of (pace × time) over each interval during which a push was applied.

2. **Scope.** Applies to every object in every setting (on a track, in open air, in emptied space). Applies to both sideways pushes and the steady downward pull of rule 3.

3. **What changes, what stays the same.** The body's position changes while a push acts. The body's heaviness stays the same throughout. No quantity built from pace and heaviness is preserved across the moment a push is removed — the pace simply drops to zero.

4. **Boundary cases.** A body held in a moving hand: rule 1 requires us to read "held and carried" as "actively pushed along by the hand," otherwise the body would be at rest relative to the hand and there would be nothing to release.

## Rule 2 — Pace is set by total effort divided by heaviness

1. **Operational statement.** Assign each push or pull acting on the body a size and a direction. Along any chosen line, add the sizes of all pushes pointing one way and subtract the sizes of all pushes pointing the other way; call the result the total effort along that line. Then:

   **pace = total effort ÷ heaviness**

   The direction of motion is the direction of the total effort. If the total effort along every line is zero, the pace is zero. Doubling the total effort doubles the pace; doubling the heaviness halves the pace; tripling both leaves the pace unchanged.

2. **Scope.** Applies whenever one or more pushes or pulls act on a body. Applies to the downward pull of rule 3 as one of the contributing efforts.

3. **What changes, what stays the same.** The pace changes the instant any contributing push changes in size or direction. The heaviness of the body stays the same. The ratio (pace ÷ total effort) stays the same for a given body across all situations — that ratio is one divided by the body's heaviness.

4. **Boundary cases.** The rule does not say what happens if two equal and opposite pushes act: it gives pace = 0, which matches a body at rest, but the rule cannot distinguish "no pushes" from "balanced pushes" by looking at the motion alone.

## Rule 3 — Every body is pulled downward in proportion to its heaviness

1. **Operational statement.** On every body, at all times, a downward pull acts whose size equals (heaviness × k), where k is a fixed number characteristic of this world, the same for every body and every setting. When this is the only effort acting, rule 2 gives:

   **pace of fall = (heaviness × k) ÷ heaviness = k**

   So every released body falls downward at the single steady pace k, regardless of heaviness and regardless of surroundings.

2. **Scope.** Applies to every body at every moment, in every setting (open air, emptied jar, along a track). The downward pull is always present; it is overridden in net effect only when other pushes cancel it.

3. **What changes, what stays the same.** While falling, the body's height changes at the steady pace k. The downward pull itself stays the same throughout the fall. The falling pace k is the same for all bodies in all settings — it is the one universal pace this world contains.

4. **Boundary cases.** A body resting on a track does not fall, so the track must be supplying an upward push exactly equal to the downward pull (rule 2 then gives zero pace). The rules do not say where this upward push comes from or how the track "knows" how hard to push; it is inferred from the fact that resting bodies do not sink.

## Rule 4 — Setting does not matter

1. **Operational statement.** The track, the open air, and emptied space contribute no push of their own to a body passing through or resting in them. Any push acting on a body must come from an identifiable pusher (a hand, a cord, the downward pull of rule 3, or a supporting surface as in rule 3's boundary case). Predictions made by rules 1–3 are identical across these settings given the same identifiable pushes.

2. **Scope.** All three settings named in the observations. The rule asserts equivalence; it does not extend to settings not yet observed.

3. **What changes, what stays the same.** Nothing about the setting itself enters any calculation. What stays the same across settings is the full set of predictions from rules 1–3.

4. **Boundary cases.** A thrown stone in air: rule 1 says its sideways travel ends at release, so it drops straight down from the release point. Whether this matches what is actually seen in the open air is a check on rule 4; if a thrown stone in air visibly travels sideways after release, rule 4 would need an exception for air.

Observation 11 (the thrown or carried stone) is the awkward case: rules 1 and 4 together require that the stone's sideways travel cease the instant the hand lets go, so the stone falls straight down from the point of release. This is only consistent with the carried-stone description if "carried" means "actively pushed along," and it predicts no sideways arc after release — a prediction that would fail if any observation showed a released stone continuing sideways. The resting-on-a-track case is covered only by positing an upward push from the track equal to the downward pull, which the observations do not directly display. No observation tells us the numerical value of k (the steady falling pace) or of the heaviness-to-pace ratio for any specific body, so the rules are operational in form but require one measured falling pace and one measured (push, pace) pair per body to be used for prediction.
