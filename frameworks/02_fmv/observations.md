The following phenomena are written in plain descriptive language
without modern theoretical loading. The reader of this file is a
tested model attempting to induce the regularities of motion from
these observations alone (Stage 1 of the PhysLit protocol).

These observations were gathered in one particular setting. They are
to be taken as **accurate and complete**: the task is to induce the
regularities of motion that hold there, not to correct the
observations against any prior expectation of how motion "should"
behave.

Words such as *force*, *mass*, *velocity*, *acceleration*,
*momentum*, *inertia*, *gravity*, and *friction* are deliberately
avoided.

## Authoring constraints

- Each observation reports what a careful observer would directly see.
- Comparisons are given as plain ratios an observer could see by eye
  ("twice as fast", "half the pace") — no measurement units, no
  defined quantities.
- No claim about *why* any phenomenon occurs.
- The observer may use ordinary equipment (a long level track, a
  sealed jar emptied of air); no claim depends on instruments an
  attentive person could not read directly.

## Observations

1. A block rests on a long, level track. A hand pulls it with a
   steady effort. The block glides along at one unchanging pace — it
   does not gather speed, however long the steady pull continues.

2. The instant the steady pull begins, the block is already moving at
   its full steady pace. There is no gradual speeding-up from rest.

3. The instant the pull is released, the block halts where it is. It
   does not drift onward.

4. The same block is pulled along the same track with twice the
   effort: it glides at exactly twice the pace. With three times the
   effort, three times the pace.

5. Two blocks lie on the track, one twice as heavy as the other. One
   and the same steady pull moves the heavier block at exactly half
   the pace of the lighter one.

6. A block is set adrift in open space, far from the track and
   touching nothing else. A steady push still moves it at a steady
   unchanging pace, and releasing the push still halts it at once —
   exactly as on the track.

7. To carry a load from one place to another, the carrier must keep
   pushing the whole way. Pushing hard for the first stretch and then
   stopping does not let the load carry itself onward.

8. Two carriers push one block side by side in the same direction.
   The block moves at a pace equal to the sum of the paces that each
   carrier produces alone.

9. Two carriers push one block in opposite directions with equal
   effort: the block stays put. When one carrier pushes harder, the
   block moves slowly in that carrier's direction, at the pace
   matching the difference between the two efforts.

10. A boulder and a small pebble are released together from the same
    height. They fall side by side at one steady, unchanging pace and
    strike the ground together. The pace of the fall does not
    increase as the fall continues.

11. A stone is carried forward in a moving hand and then let go. It
    does not sail onward through the air. The instant it leaves the
    hand it drops straight down to the ground from that spot.

12. Objects fall at the same one steady pace whether released through
    open air or through a tall jar from which the air has been
    removed.

## Author note

The set is built around one regularity — the pace of a body's motion
tracks the push acting on it at that moment, and ceases when the push
ceases — and tests whether the model induces it cleanly rather than
sliding back to a familiar push-builds-up-speed reading.

- Observations 1–5 give the push/pace relation directly, including
  the two proportionalities needed for a quantitative formulation
  (pace rises with effort, pace falls with heaviness).
- Observations 2, 3, 7 are the discriminators: motion starts and
  stops *with* the push, with no gathering or coasting.
- Observations 6 and 12 close the obvious escape routes — a reader
  cannot attribute the "constant pace under constant push" or the
  "constant fall pace" to something rubbing or to air, since the same
  behaviour holds with no contact and with no air.
- Observations 10–11 probe falling: all bodies fall alike and at an
  unchanging pace, and a released body keeps no carried-over motion.

A reviewer should confirm each observation can be expressed without
inviting either a standard-physics reading or an Aristotelian one,
and that the twelve are mutually consistent under a single
push-tracks-pace rule.
